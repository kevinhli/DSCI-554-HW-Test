Policy Ripple -- Glossary / Cheat Sheet
(Internal Reference Only -- Not for the Paper)

========================================
INSTITUTIONS & DATA SOURCES
========================================

The Fed / Federal Reserve
  The central bank of the United States. Sets interest rates and controls monetary policy.

FOMC (Federal Open Market Committee)
  The Fed's policy-making body. Meets ~8 times per year to decide whether to raise, lower, or hold interest rates.

FRED
  Federal Reserve Economic Data. A free public database of 800,000+ economic time series, maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis.

========================================
RATES & YIELDS
========================================

Federal Funds Rate (DFF)
  The interest rate banks charge each other for overnight loans. This is the rate the Fed directly controls -- the "center of the ripple."

Fed Funds Target Range (DFEDTARU / DFEDTARL)
  The upper and lower bounds the Fed sets for the Federal Funds Rate. The actual rate (DFF) floats within this band.

Treasury Yields (DGS2, DGS10, DGS30)
  Interest rates on U.S. government bonds at 2-year, 10-year, and 30-year maturities. Considered "risk-free" rates; everything else is priced relative to these.

Yield Curve Spread (T10Y2Y)
  The difference between the 10-year and 2-year Treasury yields. When this goes negative ("inverted yield curve"), it historically signals a coming recession.

Baa Corporate Bond Yield (BAA)
  The interest rate on corporate bonds rated Baa by Moody's (the lowest tier of "investment grade"). Shows how much more companies pay to borrow compared to the government.

Bank Prime Rate (DPRIME)
  The base interest rate banks offer their most creditworthy customers. Moves almost in lockstep with the Fed Funds Rate.

30-Year Mortgage Rate (MORTGAGE30US)
  The average interest rate on a 30-year fixed-rate home loan. Directly affects housing affordability.

========================================
MONEY SUPPLY
========================================

M2 Money Stock (M2SL)
  A measure of all the money in circulation -- cash, checking accounts, savings accounts, money market funds, and small CDs. When the Fed does "quantitative easing" (printing money / buying bonds), M2 expands. When they tighten, it contracts.

========================================
MARKET INDICES
========================================

S&P 500 (^GSPC)
  An index tracking the 500 largest U.S. public companies. The most widely used benchmark for overall U.S. stock market performance.

Dow Jones Industrial Average (^DJI)
  An index of 30 large U.S. companies. Older and narrower than the S&P 500 but still widely cited.

VIX (^VIX)
  The "fear index." Measures expected stock market volatility over the next 30 days. Spikes during uncertainty (e.g., surprise rate decisions, crises).

========================================
ETFs (EXCHANGE-TRADED FUNDS)
========================================

ETF
  A fund that trades on a stock exchange like a regular stock but holds a basket of assets (stocks, bonds, commodities). Lets you buy exposure to a whole category in one ticker.

GLD -- SPDR Gold Shares ETF
  Tracks the price of gold. Gold is a classic "safe haven" -- investors buy it when they're worried about inflation or instability.

VEU -- Vanguard FTSE All-World ex-US ETF
  Tracks stock markets in every developed country except the U.S. Shows how Fed decisions spill over globally.

EEM -- iShares MSCI Emerging Markets ETF
  Tracks stocks in developing economies (China, India, Brazil, etc.). Emerging markets are especially sensitive to Fed rate changes because higher U.S. rates pull investment capital out of these countries.

XLF -- Financial Select Sector SPDR
  Tracks banks and financial companies. Banks profit more when rates are higher (wider lending margins).

XLRE -- Real Estate Select Sector SPDR
  Tracks real estate companies/REITs. Higher rates hurt real estate (more expensive mortgages = less buying).

XLK -- Technology Select Sector SPDR
  Tracks tech companies. Tech stocks are sensitive to rates because their valuations depend heavily on future earnings discounted at the current rate.

XLE -- Energy Select Sector SPDR
  Tracks oil/gas/energy companies. Less directly tied to rates but affected by economic growth expectations.

XLU -- Utilities Select Sector SPDR
  Tracks utility companies. Utilities carry heavy debt, so higher rates increase their borrowing costs. Also competes with bonds for income-seeking investors.

========================================
ALTERNATIVE ASSETS
========================================

Bitcoin (BTC-USD)
  A decentralized digital currency. Its relationship with Fed policy is debated -- sometimes it behaves like "digital gold" (inflation hedge), other times like a high-risk tech stock. During 2022 rate hikes, it crashed ~65%.

========================================
ECONOMIC INDICATORS
========================================

Unemployment Rate (UNRATE)
  Percentage of the labor force that is jobless and actively looking for work.

Nonfarm Payrolls (PAYEMS)
  Total number of paid U.S. workers excluding farm workers. The single most watched monthly jobs number.

CPI (CPIAUCSL)
  Consumer Price Index. Measures inflation by tracking the average price of a basket of consumer goods (food, rent, gas, etc.).

PCE Price Index (PCEPI)
  Personal Consumption Expenditures Price Index. The Fed's preferred inflation measure (slightly different methodology than CPI, tends to run lower).

Housing Starts (HOUST)
  Number of new residential construction projects started each month. A leading indicator for the housing market and broader economy.

========================================
OTHER TERMS
========================================

Basis Point (bp)
  1/100th of a percentage point. A 25bp rate hike means rates went up by 0.25%. Financial people say "basis points" instead of percentages to avoid ambiguity.

Quantitative Easing (QE)
  When the Fed buys bonds (and other assets) to inject money into the economy. Expands M2. Used during crises (2008, 2020).

Quantitative Tightening (QT)
  The reverse of QE -- the Fed lets bonds mature or sells them, pulling money out of the economy. Contracts M2.

Sankey Diagram
  A flow diagram where the width of arrows/ribbons is proportional to the quantity flowing. Used in economics to show money moving between sectors.

Z-score Normalization
  A way to make different datasets comparable by converting each value to "how many standard deviations is this from the mean?" Lets you put unemployment (measured in %) and S&P 500 (measured in points) on the same scale.
